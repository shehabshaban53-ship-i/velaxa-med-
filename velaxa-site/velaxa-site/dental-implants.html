Dental implants

Implant treatment starts with diagnostics, bone support, and the final restoration plan.

Implant planning is not limited to replacing a missing tooth root. The case must be reviewed for bone volume, gum condition, bite, adjacent teeth, and whether the final restoration will be a single crown, multiple units, or a wider implant-supported solution.

What implants can address

Different implant cases need different planning depth and different timelines.

Single missing tooth

A single implant may replace one missing tooth and support one final crown when the surrounding bone and bite permit it.

Multiple missing teeth

More than one implant may be needed when several teeth are missing and the prosthetic plan requires wider support.

Full-arch rehabilitation

Selected cases may need a broader implant-supported plan across the full arch after imaging and bite review.

Who may be a suitable candidate

Implant suitability depends on the support available for both surgery and the final bite.

  • Bone volume and density

    Enough bone support is needed for stable placement. Some cases can move directly to implant treatment, while others may need grafting or staged healing.

  • Gum condition

    Healthy soft tissue supports a more stable restorative result and a more predictable long-term maintenance plan.

  • Bite and neighboring teeth

    Implants should be planned with the opposing teeth and the final bite in mind, not as an isolated surgical decision.

  • Medical and dental history

    Past extractions, previous restorations, bruxism, and general healing factors may all affect timing and treatment sequencing.

Diagnostics and planning

The first useful implant discussion usually comes from photos, scans, and restoration goals together.

Imaging review

Panoramic imaging and, when needed, more detailed scans are used to understand bone condition and possible implant position.

Prosthetic direction

The implant stage and the final crown or bridge stage should be planned together so function and aesthetics stay aligned.

Staging decision

Some cases move faster, while others need extraction healing, grafting, or delayed loading before the final restoration stage.

Visit structure

The patient should understand whether the case is likely to involve one main visit or a staged treatment path before travel is fixed.

Implant stages

The treatment path is easier to evaluate when the stages are separated clearly.

1. Consultation and imaging

The case starts with smile photos, X-rays, scan review, and the identification of the missing-tooth pattern.

2. Surgical planning

Bone support, gum condition, implant position, and any need for grafting or staged healing are discussed.

3. Implant placement

The surgical phase focuses on stable positioning that supports the planned final restoration.

4. Healing phase

Selected cases need time for osseointegration before the definitive restorative stage begins.

5. Prosthetic stage

The final crown, bridge, or full-arch restoration is designed for fit, function, and appearance.

6. Maintenance

Long-term success depends on hygiene, review visits, and the condition of the surrounding tissue over time.

FAQ

Questions that usually come before implant booking.

What files should I send for the first implant review?

Smile photos, intraoral photos, and a panoramic X-ray are the most useful starting point. Any older treatment record can help.

Can implant treatment be staged?

Yes. Some cases move quickly, while others need healing time, grafting, or a staged prosthetic sequence after surgery.

Does every case need a scan?

Not always at the first message, but detailed imaging is often needed before the surgical stage is confirmed properly.

Can I start on WhatsApp and send scans later?

Yes. The initial contact can begin on WhatsApp, and the team will tell you what additional imaging is still needed.
